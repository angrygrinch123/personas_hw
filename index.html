Contactless Water Dispenser

This is a contextual inquiry on a contactless water dispenser on the fourth floor of the CIT. The purpose of the interface is to reduce contact between people via objects to reduce the spread the COVID-19.

"Quench" Interface Sketch

This is a sketch of the "Quench" interface. Water dispenses if you hover your hand over the sensor. Hovering over the red outlined sensor will dispense hot water. Sensors are used to reduce contact with the actual dispenser.

Key Observations
  • Users' natural instinct is to click or press the top screen or the "sensors" for water
  • Users hesitate and start to fiddle with the cup/watter bottle to trigger a non-existent sensor under when pressing the button doesn't work very well
  • One user placed the cup under the dispenser and waited for something to happen, only to realize that nothiing happened and thought the machine was broken
  • Even after finding out how the hover sensor works, some users give up mid way (i.e. fill their water bottle halfway)

User Interviews

Investigating user behavior, thoughts, and emotions

Interview Questions
  1. Have you ever used a contactless water dispenser before?
  2. Do you think the instructions below helped?
  3. Was there anything that you liked or bothered you about using your hand to use the dispenser? If there were things that bothered you, how would you change them?
  4. Do you prefer to use the regular faucet over this dispenser? If so, why? If not, why not?
  5. Do you think the dispenser is convenient?
Interview Responses
  • All 3 users have used a contactless water dispenser before at the Nelson but not one that required hand interaction
  • Only 1 of the user read the instruction below and was able to find out how to use it while other users preferred trial-and-error because there was so much text
  • Users find it uncomfortable and unnatural to use their hand to dispense water because it restricts their movement and induce feeling of silliness in public
  • All users think a better design for contactless water dispensing are like the ones in the Nelson where the water bottle is placed below for water, but would use this dispenser again for "the fun of it"
  • All three users did not think the dispenser was extremely convenient, because they could not use the dispenser for long periods of time because their arms would hurt, but would be fine to use it just to fill a mug or a water bottle

Personas & Empathy Maps

Understanding and empathizing with users

Busy Bee Beatrice - Persona 1

Busy Bee Beatrice is always on-the-run because she is a Brown undergraduate junior who takes four STEM classes, does research, is looking for a job, and leads the lacorsse team so time is not something she has.

Beatrice is filling up her water bottle right before her practice and cannot be late because she is the team captain. Yet, she struggles to get water and ignores the additional directions written, and keeps clicking on the fountain because she is too preocuppied. She usually multi-tasks and responds to emails while filling up water, but she can't do that with a interface that requires her to constantly put her hand up. Beatrice represents users' frustrations with the interface and the consequences of poor, unintuitive design interfaces, in constrained, real-life settings.

Thinks

  • Is this machine broken?
  • Maybe I should go to the Nelson and get water from there?
  • Why does it pour water sometimes and not other times?

Feels

  • Frustrated that water isn't working and wasting her time
  • Confused as how the interface works
  • Feels silly that she is struggling in public

Says

  • Would you know how this works?
  • Is this machine broken?
  • Is there a fountain somewhere else in this building?
  • Is this how you do it?

Does

  • Constantly taps the button to wait for the water to come out
  • Presses hard / applies pressure on the button for water
  • Moves the water bottle left and right for water to come, thinking the sensor is below

Slow and Steady Sam - Persona 2

Slow and Steady Sam is a middle-aged, tenured professor at Brown, who sent all his kids to college, and is cruising by and his only concerns are his slightly aching arm from the playing skyrim for a decade and trying to figure out where he should be during his sabbatical with his wife (maybe Hawaii).

Sam strolls into the CIT kitchen for tea and realizes that there is a set of instructions on the interface. Other than his coffee chat in two hours, he has no other pressing committments - he is after all going on a sabbatical in a month. So, sam takes his time and reads through each detail of the interface and smirks when he finds that he has to hold his hand like Darth Vader from Star Wars to get water. He winces a little bit because his arm hurts - maybe he should play less Skyrim (or not). Sam represents users who have time and luxury to read the instruction manuals of an interface and demonstrate users with the most positive kind of experience with the contactless water faucet. He also represents users with a temporary physical limitation that may prevent them from enjoying the full experience, but not to a damaging degree.

Thinks

  • Someone must have had a lot of questions asked to write all these instructions.
  • Fair enough, this seems pretty simple.
  • How is it reducing contact if it touches my cup?

Feels

  • Relieved, that Brown's CIT is covid-conscious
  • Amused that someone had to write a long set of instructions for a simple sensor
  • Entertained to be Darth Vader

Says

  • Someone must have really been mad writing these instructions?
  • May the force be with you
  • Oof can't do this too long - my arm hurts
  • Oops, that's cold water

Does

  • Correctly places hand in front of the red sensor region, but first goes right above the cold water because he's right-handed
  • Winces because his arm hurts, and switches arm for hot water
  • Chuckles because someone wrote really long instructions

Storyboard

Reliving Sam's Experience

Sam realizes he would like some hot tea. He has tea, but not hot water.

He goes to the CIT kitchen and realizes there's a new water dispenser. He pauses to see how to use it.

He realizes someone wrote a long set of instructions on how to use the water dispenser.

Sam places the cup first under the dispenser. He places it so that it fits the center where the water may dispense from.

Sam hovers his hand to the right, because he's a right-handed person. Naturally he hovers over cold water.

Sam realizes he actually needs hot water and quickly adjust his arm to the left to hover over the red sensor.

Sam is amused to obtain not water by hovering his hand, instead of pressing a button. He feels like Darth Vader.

After some time, Sam winces, because he has a bad arm. His years of playing Skyrim and sitting at his desk coding has created acute shoulder pain.

So Sam switches arms, and uses his left arm to continue obtaining water.

Sam is content because now he has hot tea in a germ-free manner.
